Rotorcrafts - Rotary Wing Aircraft

A rotorcraft or rotary-wing aircraft is a heavier-than-air flying machine that uses lift generated by wings, called rotary wings or rotor blades, that revolve around a mast.

Usage:

Transportation of people and cargo, military uses, construction, firefighting, search and rescue, tourism, medical transport, law enforcement, agriculture, news and media, and aerial observation.

Parts:

Fuselage:

The entire framework of the helicopter is referred to as the fuselage. This frame is created using very strong and durable materials. However, for flight to be possible, these materials have to be very lightweight. To this end, the fuselage is made using composite materials.

Engines:

Helicopters will have one or two engines depending on the type. Some small helicopters still use piston engines. However, modern ones employ gas turbines which are like jet engines.

Main Rotor:

The big rotor that spins is the most conspicuous feature in any chopper. The role of this rotor is to create the needed force to ensure that the craft becomes airborne. Every chopper will need another rotor so as to fulfill Newtonâ€™s third law of motion. Without another rotor to a chopper, controlling it would be impossible. Therefore, the second rotor spins in the opposite direction to provide the needed control.

Tail Rotor:

A tail rotor can be used as the second rotor in a chopper that does not have a second larger rotor. It is a small propeller that often points sideways. This tail rotor can also be built inside the tail. This component is critical because it will determine the safety of the chopper.
